# Islamic Contractual Ethics According to Ahlus Sunnah wal Jama'ah (Understanding of the Salaf)

## Purpose

This document provides a supplementary Islamic ethical explanation of the seriousness of agreements, permissions, conditions, and trust-based obligations related to licensing and commercial permissions.

It does not replace legal review, statutory compliance, or the binding legal text of the license. Instead, it explains why honoring agreements is a serious moral and religious responsibility in Islam according to **Ahlus Sunnah wal Jama'ah upon the understanding of the Salafus Salih**.

## Methodological Foundation

Ahlus Sunnah wal Jama'ah return matters of belief, worship, character, dealings, rights, and obligations to:

- the Qur'an;
- the authentic Sunnah;
- and the understanding of the Salaf from the Sahabah, the Tabi'in, and those who followed them in goodness.

Accordingly, contracts, permissions, trusts, and property rights are not treated as casual or merely technical matters. They are part of **`'uqud`** (binding agreements), **amanah** (trust), **'ahd** (covenant), truthfulness, justice, and fear of Allah.

## Core Principle

In Islam, when a person knowingly accepts terms, conditions, restrictions, permissions, or boundaries, that person becomes morally responsible before Allah to honor them truthfully and fairly, as long as the agreement itself does not require disobedience to Allah.

From the manhaj of the Salaf, a Muslim should not seek loopholes to justify wrongdoing, betrayal, deception, or unlawful gain. Rather, the believer is commanded to fulfill rights, preserve trust, and avoid ظلم (wrongdoing), خيانة (betrayal), غدر (treachery), and أكل المال بالباطل (wrongful consumption of wealth).

## Qur'anic Foundations

### 1. Fulfill Contracts and Covenants

The Qur'an commands believers to fulfill agreements and covenants. This is a foundational principle in dealings and is not treated lightly by the people of Sunnah.

Relevant references include:

- Surah Al-Ma'idah 5:1
- Surah Al-Isra' 17:34
- Surah An-Nahl 16:91

### 2. Render Trusts Properly and Do Not Betray Them

Licenses, permissions, access rights, and usage limits may fall under matters of trust. To knowingly exceed agreed permission, conceal misuse, or exploit someone else's property beyond granted terms may enter into betrayal of trust.

Relevant references include:

- Surah Al-Anfal 8:27
- Surah An-Nisa' 4:58

### 3. Do Not Consume Wealth Unjustly

If a person takes commercial benefit while knowingly ignoring agreed restrictions, this may fall under consuming wealth or benefit in a false, unjust, or prohibited manner.

Relevant references include:

- Surah An-Nisa' 4:29
- Surah Al-Baqarah 2:188

### 4. Record and Clarify Obligations Properly

The Qur'an teaches the importance of careful documentation where rights, duties, and future disputes may arise. This supports clarity in licensing, permissions, commercial exceptions, and written approvals.

Relevant reference:

- Surah Al-Baqarah 2:282

### 5. Accountability for Small and Great Deeds

A person may assume that contractual misuse is small if no lawsuit occurs, no public dispute appears, or no one notices. However, the Qur'an teaches that every deed, even what people consider small, is accounted for before Allah.

Relevant references include:

- Surah Az-Zalzalah 99:7-8
- Surah Al-Kahf 18:49

## Understanding This Through the Way of the Salaf

According to Ahlus Sunnah wal Jama'ah:

1. **Rights must be returned to their owners.** Property, access, permission, and benefit are not taken beyond what is lawfully granted.
2. **Contracts are to be honored.** A believer does not agree outwardly and then violate inwardly.
3. **Trust is a religious duty.** Amanah is not only a social value but part of obedience to Allah.
4. **Worldly cleverness does not justify sin.** Reframing commercial use as "educational," "testing," or "personal" while intending commercial gain is deception, not piety.
5. **The unseen accountability is real.** Even hidden misuse remains known to Allah.
6. **Judgment must remain just.** Ahlus Sunnah do not make reckless declarations about specific individuals' final state, but they affirm that betrayal, injustice, and wrongful consumption are grave sins that expose a person to punishment unless Allah forgives him.

## Responsibilities of a Muslim User or Maintainer

A Muslim dealing with contracts, licenses, permissions, or commercial restrictions should take the following responsibilities seriously:

1. Read and understand the terms before acting.
2. Do not claim rights that were not granted.
3. Do not hide commercial intent behind personal, educational, charitable, or experimental labels.
4. Seek permission clearly when commercial use is intended.
5. Preserve honesty in attribution, scope of use, and modification history.
6. Avoid exploitation, deception, manipulation, and wrongful financial gain.
7. Document approvals and exceptions in writing to prevent injustice and dispute.
8. Rectify violations quickly by stopping misuse, seeking permission, compensating rights where necessary, and making sincere repentance to Allah.

## Risks of Violating Agreements

Violating an agreement is not only a legal risk. In the understanding of Ahlus Sunnah wal Jama'ah, it may also create moral and spiritual harm, including:

- betrayal of trust
- false representation
- breaking a covenant after accepting it
- consuming wealth or benefit unjustly
- nurturing hypocrisy in conduct through dishonesty and double-dealing
- becoming answerable before Allah for deliberate misuse and wrongful gain

## Consequences in the Hereafter

According to Qur'anic principles affirmed by Ahlus Sunnah wal Jama'ah, a person who knowingly violates agreements, betrays trust, or takes wrongful benefit should fear accountability in the Hereafter. Even if worldly consequences are delayed, hidden, minimized, or avoided, Allah knows the reality of every promise, intention, condition, and act.

This means:

- hidden misuse is not hidden from Allah;
- wrongful gain is not spiritually harmless;
- covenant-breaking is not a trivial matter;
- betrayal of trust is not excused by technical cleverness;
- every act tied to truth, trust, and property will be judged with perfect justice.

The believer should therefore approach contracts with **taqwa**, sidq (truthfulness), wara' (scrupulous caution), and khawf from Allah's reckoning, while hoping for Allah's mercy through honesty, repentance, and restitution.

## Important Theological Clarification

This document should be understood with balance:

- We affirm the seriousness of sins related to betrayal, injustice, and wrongful consumption.
- We do not declare specific individuals to be disbelievers merely for contractual violations unless there is clear evidence for a matter that reaches disbelief according to the principles of Ahlus Sunnah.
- We distinguish between sinful conduct, oppression, betrayal, and disbelief.
- Final judgment of individuals in the Hereafter belongs to Allah.

This balanced approach is more in line with the methodology of the Salaf: affirm the texts, warn against sin, preserve justice, and avoid excess.

## Practical Ethical Guidance

For maintainers, users, and commercial applicants, this means:

- be transparent about intended use;
- ask before using beyond the granted scope;
- do not reinterpret restrictions dishonestly;
- do not assume silence means permission;
- do not monetize what is restricted without written approval;
- correct mistakes quickly if misuse has occurred;
- seek halal, lawful, and clearly permitted commercial arrangements;
- remember that written permission in worldly terms may also protect one from wrongdoing in religious terms.
